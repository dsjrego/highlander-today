# Monetization Plan — Highlander Today

> **Last updated:** 2026-03-28
> **Status:** Strategic direction document. Not a current build priority; intended to guide decisions as the platform matures and expands to additional communities.

## Foundational Principle

Highlander Today is community infrastructure. It should behave more like a utility than a commercial product. Monetization must reinforce that identity, not undermine it. The core test for any revenue mechanism is: does the person paying understand what they're paying for, and does the community benefit from the transaction?

Revenue should come from services that create obvious value above and beyond basic community participation, and from entities that derive business value from the platform. Residents using the platform for its core purpose — staying informed, finding things, connecting with neighbors — should never feel like they're being charged for access to their own community.

## The Disruption Thesis

The internet platforms that displaced local institutions all followed the same playbook: aggregate local information, strip out local identity and trust, monetize attention through advertising, and let the underlying community erode. The result is a landscape where local life is mediated by companies that have no stake in the places they serve.

Zillow scrapes MLS data and sells leads back to the agents who created the listings. Indeed and ZipRecruiter do the same with job postings. Yelp holds businesses hostage with reviews they cannot control. Facebook groups replaced community forums but gave communities zero ownership, zero data, and an algorithm optimized for engagement rather than local value. Craigslist democratized classifieds but built zero trust infrastructure, which is why every interaction there carries a background risk of fraud or worse. Legacy.com charges grieving families to interact with their own loved one's memorial page. Local newspapers, already weakened, charge $400-$600 to print an obituary that disappears from public view within days.

These platforms are vulnerable because they built scale without trust, and they extract value without returning it to the communities they serve.

Highlander Today inverts this. The trust layer — real people, vouched for, with persistent identity and accountability — is something none of these platforms have and none of them can build, because their business models depend on anonymous, frictionless access at massive scale. What they treat as friction, we treat as the product.

The strategic opportunity is not to compete with these platforms on their terms. It is to make them irrelevant at the local level by providing something they structurally cannot: trusted, community-owned infrastructure where the value stays local.

### Where the Incumbents Are Vulnerable

**Real estate.** Zillow and Realtor.com are aggregators with no local knowledge and no trust. A community-rooted property listing with verified local sellers, auto-generated neighborhood context from the platform's own data, and trusted messaging between real people is a fundamentally different experience. Agents pay Zillow for leads that Zillow scraped from them. Highlander can offer agents direct community presence at a fraction of the cost and actually connect them with real neighbors rather than anonymous clicks.

**Job boards.** Indeed charges employers and gives workers nothing. The Help Wanted board already has something Indeed never will — the poster and the applicant are both real, vouched-for community members. For local jobs, service work, and gigs, that matters more than reach. A restaurant owner in Cambria Heights does not need 200 applicants from Indeed; they need three trustworthy local people.

**Obituaries and memorials.** Legacy.com charges families to interact with their own loved one's memorial. Newspapers charge hundreds of dollars for a few column inches that disappear. Highlander offers something permanent, community-owned, and dignified — with steward controls, verification, and moderation that respect the family rather than exploiting the moment.

**Local business discovery.** Google Business and Yelp control how local businesses are found, and they extract rent for that control. The store and marketplace system, combined with the planned directory and organization foundation, gives businesses a presence that is inside the community rather than mediated by an outside platform that does not care about them.

**Classifieds and local commerce.** Facebook Marketplace and Craigslist are trust-free zones. The Highlander marketplace requires real identity. That single difference changes everything about the quality and safety of the interaction.

**Community information.** Patch, Nextdoor, and local Facebook groups are either ad-supported shells or engagement-optimized toxicity machines. Highlander is building an actual editorial and community information surface with moderation, accountability, and local ownership.

## Two-Tier Community Model

### Home Community (Cambria Heights) — Free

Highlander Today is free for the Cambria Heights community. This is the founding community, the proving ground, and the long-term home base. Keeping it free is both a commitment to the community and a strategic necessity: the platform must demonstrate real adoption, repeat engagement, and visible local value before it can credibly offer itself to other communities.

Free does not mean zero revenue. Value-added services (described below) can generate income even in the free home community, as long as those services are clearly above and beyond basic community participation and the costs are tied to something real and understandable.

### Tenant Communities — Paid

Subsequent communities running on the Highlander platform will pay for the service. The pricing model, subscription terms, and community onboarding process are not yet defined, but the pitch is straightforward: your community gets its own locally rooted space with trusted identity, local content, marketplace, events, help wanted, and all the infrastructure that used to require a local newspaper, a Facebook group, a Craigslist page, and a dozen disconnected tools — unified under one accountable platform that the community controls.

The home community's success is the proof point. Expansion is only credible after Cambria Heights shows visible, sustained local adoption.

## Revenue Mechanisms — Value-Added Services

These services can apply to both the free home community and paid tenant sites. They are designed so the cost maps to something real (storage, processing, premium capability) and the value is obvious to the person paying.

### Memoriam: Death Notices and Memorial Pages

The public-facing home for this content is the `Memoriam` section under the `Community` nav dropdown. See `COMMUNITY-SECTION-PLAN.md` for the full section direction and `OBITUARIES-PLAN.md` for verification, stewardship, and moderation details.

Death notices are free. A death notice is basic community information — who passed, when, where services are held. It is the kind of information a community utility should provide at no cost, and it stands in deliberate contrast to newspapers charging hundreds of dollars for essentially the same thing.

Memorial pages may carry a modest fee tied to media hosting. Photographs, video tributes, extended galleries, and long-term preservation of richer memorial content cost real money to store and serve over time. The fee covers infrastructure, not grief. Families should understand that they are paying for the permanence and richness of the memorial, not for the right to acknowledge a death in their community.

Memorial pages are intended to be permanent community records, maintained by the platform as part of local history. Over time, the Memoriam archive connects to the History surface under the same Community section and the broader "Verified Local Memory" direction.

### Store and Marketplace Presence

Basic marketplace participation is free community infrastructure. Browsing, discovery, and trusted-user messaging between buyers and sellers cost nothing.

Premium store presence is a business service. A richer storefront with expanded photo galleries, featured placement, or a custom subdomain (such as `flavortown.highlander.today`, as envisioned in the organization/directory plan) is a paid upgrade. Local businesses expect to pay for visibility and presentation tools. The key constraint is that browsing and discovery always remain free for residents — the business pays, never the buyer.

### Event Promotion

Basic event listings are free. Telling the community about an event is core coordination infrastructure.

Promoted event features are a paid tier. A church, nonprofit, or business that wants their event featured on the homepage, wants to attach downloadable flyers, or wants enhanced visibility could pay a modest amount. The line is clear: informing people about your event is free; marketing your event costs something.

### Auto-Generated Print and Distribution Materials

When someone creates an event (or potentially a marketplace listing, help wanted post, or other structured content), the platform could offer a one-click service to generate a print-ready PDF flyer or email-ready distribution version. The data already exists in the platform — event name, date, time, location, description, image. The service transforms that data into a polished, downloadable format.

The cost to produce is minimal (templated PDF generation), but the value to the user is real — it saves them the time and effort of designing a flyer in a separate tool. A small per-download fee or a modest monthly unlimited plan would feel reasonable. Churches, fire companies, school booster groups, and small businesses make flyers constantly and would benefit from skipping the design step entirely. Multiple template styles could be offered so outputs do not look uniform.

This pattern — charging for transformation of data the user already created — is a clean, non-extractive revenue model that can extend to other content types over time.

### Help Wanted Featured Posts

Posting a job, service request, or gig is free community infrastructure. Promoted or pinned placement for a defined period is a paid upgrade. This follows the Craigslist model: posting is free, but businesses that want their listing highlighted pay a small fee. The trust layer makes this more valuable than Craigslist's version because both parties are verified community members.

### Organization Pages and Directory Presence

A basic directory listing for a church, fire company, civic group, or local business is free. A managed organization page with staff rosters, event calendars, document hosting, and custom subdomain routing is a paid service. Organizations have operational budgets for this kind of presence in a way that individual residents do not. The value proposition is a complete, community-integrated web presence that replaces a standalone website, a Facebook page, and a Google Business listing with something rooted in the local trust network.

### Real Estate Services

A local real estate section could charge agents or sellers a modest posting fee for property listings. The trust layer adds real value here — listings come from verified community members, not anonymous or fraudulent sources.

Beyond basic listings, the platform can auto-generate value-added materials that no MLS or national aggregator provides. A property fact sheet PDF could include listing details, photographs, and local context pulled from the platform itself — nearby stores, upcoming community events, neighborhood highlights, community profile information. This is something a real estate agent would pay for because it makes their listing feel personal and locally grounded in a way that a Zillow page never can.

Move-in welcome packages — auto-generated guides with local stores, upcoming events, community contacts, and onboarding into the platform — could be offered to agents as closing gifts or directly to new residents for a small fee.

The broader opportunity is to make the community platform the authoritative local context layer for real estate, so that buying or selling a home in the community naturally routes through Highlander rather than through a national aggregator that strips out everything local.

### Local Creator Network

As documented in `LOCAL-CREATOR-NETWORK-PLAN.md`, the platform intends to support a local creator and shows network for podcasters, video creators, educators, musicians, churches, civic broadcasters, and other community media producers.

The creator network generates revenue through platform fees on creator monetization tools: show subscriptions, paid premieres, pay-per-view episodes, and supporter memberships. Highlander provides trusted discovery, distribution, subscription infrastructure, and audience access; creators pay a modest platform fee in exchange. This is structurally different from ad-driven creator platforms because the revenue comes from direct audience relationships, not from selling attention to advertisers.

The creator network also significantly strengthens the tenant community pitch. A single community may not generate enough programming to feel like a network, but multiple tenant communities across a region create a genuine local media catalog with real category depth. The pitch to a new tenant community becomes: your local creators get distribution across the entire network, not just your town.

Churches and faith organizations are likely the strongest early anchor for the creator network. They already produce weekly programming (sermons, music, announcements), have built-in audiences, are deeply rooted in community identity, and are generally underserved by existing platforms. A church that can host its Sunday service on its Highlander organization page, appear in the Faith programming rail, and let congregation members subscribe for notifications gets immediate, tangible value.

Basic creator discovery and show/episode listing should be free. Monetization tools (subscriptions, paid access, supporter memberships) are the paid tier, and the platform fee on those transactions is the revenue mechanism.

### Qualified Interest Marketplace (Future)

As documented in `QUALIFIED-INTEREST-MARKETPLACE-PLAN.md`, a buyer-initiated lead marketplace where residents control access to their attention, businesses pay for qualified engagement, and compensation flows primarily to the resident represents the most structurally interesting long-term revenue model. The platform takes a cut of transactions that create value for both sides. No one feels extracted from because money flows through the platform, not out of the community.

This is explicitly future work and should only be revisited after core content, commerce, and community loops are clearly working.

## Legal Notices — Long-Term Positioning

Highlander Today does not currently qualify as a publication of record for legal notices under Pennsylvania law. The legal definition requires a print publication with regular frequency, paid circulation, and typically a multi-year track record of continuous publication.

However, Pennsylvania is actively considering modernization. HB 1291 (heard by the House Local Government Committee in September 2025) would allow digital-only publications to publish legal notices in communities where no printed newspaper exists, provided the digital outlet meets qualifications including an audited subscriber base, three years in business, local staff, local governmental reporting, general-interest news coverage, and at least weekly updates.

While the current legislation is written to protect existing newspapers first — with digital publication as a fallback for communities that have lost their paper — the direction of travel is clear. Rural Pennsylvania communities are losing local papers, and someone will need to fill the legal notice role.

The platform should build the operational track record that would position it (or a tenant community site) to qualify under current or future legislation: consistent local government coverage in Local Life, regular publication cadence, verifiable editorial activity, and a documented history of community service. This is not a near-term revenue target, but it is a long-term strategic position that aligns naturally with what is already being built.

For tenant communities in townships that have lost their local paper, the legal notice question becomes part of the platform's pitch to municipal governments: your community has no qualifying publication anymore, and we can be that.

## What Is Never Charged For

Basic community participation must remain free. This includes: reading content, posting in community sections, searching, messaging between trusted members, commenting, vouching, death notices, basic event listings, basic marketplace listings, basic help wanted posts, and basic directory presence.

The moment any of these feel paywalled, the platform stops being infrastructure and starts being a product. The revenue model depends on the free tier being genuinely useful and complete — value-added services succeed because people already trust and depend on the platform, not because essential features have been artificially withheld.

The general frame: residents use it free; entities that derive business value from it pay. That is how utilities work.

## Sequencing

Monetization should not be introduced prematurely. The recommended order is:

1. Prove real engagement and repeat interaction loops in Cambria Heights with no revenue pressure.
2. If donations are introduced (per `DONATIONS-TRANSPARENCY-PLAN.md`), do so only after visible community value is established and a basic transparency surface exists.
3. Introduce value-added services (auto-generated materials, premium store presence, memorial media hosting) as natural extensions of features people are already using.
4. Define and validate tenant community pricing only after the home community demonstrates sustained adoption.
5. Introduce creator network discovery and basic show/episode listing as a free community feature; add creator monetization tools (subscriptions, paid access, platform fees) only after the creator catalog has real depth and regular programming.
6. Pursue legal notice qualification as a long-term strategic position, not a near-term revenue target.
7. Revisit the qualified-interest marketplace only after core loops are clearly working and the organization/directory foundation is in place.

Revenue follows trust, adoption, and demonstrated value — not the other way around.
