# Qualified Interest Marketplace Plan

> **Status:** Product/economic planning only. Not implemented.
> **Purpose:** Define a buyer-initiated offer system where residents control access to their attention, businesses pay for qualified engagement, and the platform operates as accountable market infrastructure rather than a traditional advertising surface.

---

## Concept Summary

This system is not conventional display advertising.

It is a qualified-interest marketplace, or lead marketplace, in which:

- a resident declares genuine interest in a product or service category
- businesses compete for the right to engage that resident
- the resident controls how much access to grant and at what stage
- the resident may receive compensation for verified engagement milestones
- the platform takes a transparent, proportionate fee for operating the system

The core idea is that buyer attention has value, and the platform should help govern access to that attention fairly instead of capturing most of the value simply because it sits in the middle.

---

## Why This Exists

Traditional advertising and lead generation often have three problems:

- users experience ads as noise, clutter, and unwanted interruption
- businesses spend money with incomplete information about real buyer intent
- platforms benefit from broad access to attention even when users do not

The proposed system tries to invert that structure:

- users opt into categories they actually care about
- businesses spend against declared demand instead of broad untargeted exposure
- users retain privacy and control over escalating access
- compensation flows primarily to the person whose time and attention are being used

---

## Core Product Framing

### For residents

- declare interest once instead of sorting through constant sales noise
- compare offers on your terms
- control who gets access to you and when
- receive limited compensation for genuine, verified engagement

This is not intended to be a way for residents to make money. Compensation exists to recognize the value of real time, attention, inconvenience, and follow-through.

### For businesses

- reach opted-in local buyers instead of relying on spray-and-pray marketing
- see clearer signals about buyer seriousness and follow-through history
- choose how much a given engagement opportunity is worth
- spend marketing money on qualified attention rather than passive impressions

### For the platform

- provide the trust, workflow, measurement, messaging, consent, and payment infrastructure
- charge a transparent, proportionate fee for operating the system
- avoid turning the product into a cluttered ad surface

---

## Economic Philosophy

The platform should not be the main beneficiary simply because it controls introductions.

Recommended principle:

- businesses pay for qualified access to real buyer attention
- most of that value should go to the buyer
- the platform may retain a transparent fee to support technology, operations, trust/safety, payouts, moderation, and dispute handling

The platform should avoid vague claims such as "low fee" unless it can justify them. The better standard is:

- transparent fee structure
- proportionate fee relative to value provided and operating costs
- visible explanation of what the fee supports

---

## Interaction Model

The system should be buyer-initiated.

Typical flow:

1. A resident creates an intent posting for a category such as real estate, auto purchase, contractor services, insurance, or another high-consideration local need.
2. The resident provides structured criteria such as budget, timing, location, preferences, and constraints.
3. Approved businesses review the opportunity and decide whether to submit an offer or request access.
4. The resident reviews offers and controls whether a business may escalate to deeper engagement stages.
5. Compensation, if any, is tied to verified engagement milestones rather than generic exposure.

This is best understood as a controlled offer-exchange system, not a banner-ad system.

---

## Progressive Consent Gateways

The marketplace should treat access as layered consent rather than all-or-nothing exposure.

Illustrative gateway ladder:

1. `View Offer`
   - the resident is open to reviewing an offer
   - no direct contact information is revealed
   - compensation may be zero

2. `Anonymous Conversation`
   - the resident allows messaging inside the platform
   - no direct contact details are revealed
   - compensation may still be zero

3. `Address Shared`
   - the resident allows address disclosure for location-dependent quotes or services
   - small compensation may apply

4. `Phone Shared`
   - the resident allows direct phone contact
   - greater compensation may apply

5. `Video Call`
   - the resident agrees to a scheduled remote meeting
   - stronger compensation may apply

6. `In-Person Appointment`
   - the resident agrees to show up for a verified meeting or appointment
   - highest compensation tier

Guiding rules:

- every escalation step should require explicit resident consent
- categories may use different subsets of these gateways
- compensation should increase only when the buyer's time commitment and exposure meaningfully increase
- payment is for verified consideration and engagement, not for agreeing to purchase

---

## Compensation Model Direction

Recommended principles:

- businesses decide what a given opportunity or gateway escalation is worth
- the resident sees the offered compensation before accepting the next step
- the platform fee is disclosed separately
- completed milestones trigger compensation
- compensation may be reduced, delayed, or suspended when patterns suggest abuse

Important distinction:

- the resident is not being paid to buy
- the resident is being paid to grant verified, opted-in consideration

---

## Reputation and Quality Model

This system only works if both sides are measurable and accountable.

The marketplace should treat this as a two-sided reputation system, not a one-sided review feature.

Recommended reputation dimensions:

- `BuyerReliability`
  - does the resident respond, show up, and follow through?

- `BuyerFairness`
  - does the resident rate providers in a credible, proportionate, non-retaliatory way?

- `ProviderReliability`
  - does the business respond professionally, honor commitments, and follow through?

- `ProviderFairness`
  - does the business rate buyers honestly, or punish them for comparison-shopping, declining, or leaving justified criticism?

Recommended design rules:

- reputation should be category-aware where practical
- rating influence should be earned over time through completed interactions
- no single interaction should dominate a reputation score
- repeated retaliatory, manipulative, or extreme behavior should reduce rating weight
- both sides should have reputation consequences for persistent bad-faith behavior

---

## Funnel and Reliability Signals

Businesses should not spend blind.

The system may expose summarized intent-quality signals such as:

- offer-open rate
- response rate
- escalation acceptance rate
- phone-share rate
- call acceptance rate
- appointment show rate
- no-show rate
- verified purchase or completion rate where measurable

These signals should help businesses decide whether an opportunity is worth the spend.

Important framing:

- this is not meant to become creepy behavioral surveillance
- businesses should generally see summarized quality indicators, tiers, or category-specific funnel signals rather than a raw behavioral dossier

---

## Neutral vs Negative Outcomes

The system must distinguish between neutral non-conversion and bad conduct.

Examples of outcomes that should usually be neutral:

- buyer declines because the offer is out of budget
- buyer chooses a competing provider
- buyer decides not to proceed after honest comparison

Examples of outcomes that may be negative:

- provider uses bait-and-switch pricing
- provider misrepresents the offer
- buyer repeatedly accepts compensated stages with no serious follow-through
- either side no-shows, retaliates, harasses, or behaves dishonestly

This distinction is critical so the marketplace does not unfairly punish premium or high-price providers merely because they are expensive.

---

## Abuse and Fairness Controls

The system should assume both sides may attempt to game it.

Recommended controls:

- trusted-user or equivalent gating for higher-value resident participation
- approved/verified business participation
- rate limits on intent creation and pitch volume
- category-specific participation rules
- staged payout release or anti-fraud holds
- auditable consent events for every gateway escalation
- double-blind or delayed review reveal where appropriate
- dispute and moderation process for obvious abuse
- reduced rating weight for raters with repeated extreme or retaliatory patterns

The product should protect both residents from manipulative businesses and businesses from toxic tire-kicker behavior.

---

## Category Fit

This model is strongest where:

- the purchase is high-consideration
- the seller economics can support paying for serious engagement
- funnel stages are easy to verify
- comparison-shopping is normal

Likely strong categories:

- real estate agents and brokerages
- auto purchase
- contractors and home services
- insurance quotes
- internet/telecom service
- other high-value local professional services

Likely weak categories:

- low-ticket retail
- commodity purchases with minimal consideration
- categories where engagement milestones are difficult to verify

---

## Recommended MVP Posture

If this concept is pursued, the MVP should stay narrow.

Recommended MVP constraints:

- launch in one or two categories only
- use a limited set of gateways
- require explicit opt-in at every escalation step
- keep compensation rules simple
- expose only high-signal reputation summaries
- manually review abuse patterns early rather than over-automating immediately

The product should validate whether businesses will pay for controlled local demand and whether residents will use the system honestly before broadening the category footprint.

---

## Positioning Notes

This concept should generally not be presented as:

- banner advertising
- "get paid to watch ads"
- generic lead generation
- a side-hustle opportunity for residents

It is better framed as:

- a qualified-interest marketplace
- a buyer-controlled local offer exchange
- a consent-based lead marketplace
- infrastructure for fair, measurable access to local demand

- How much reputation detail should each side see?
- Should businesses pay at request time, unlock time, or milestone completion time?
- What exact behaviors reduce payout eligibility for residents?
- What exact behaviors reduce rating weight for either side?
