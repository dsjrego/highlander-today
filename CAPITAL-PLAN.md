# Capital Plan — Highlander Today

> **Last updated:** 2026-04-05
> **Status:** Strategic direction document. This is a capital-planning model, not a committed budget.

## Core Thesis

Highlander Today should not rely on any single funding source.

The recommended capital structure is a blended model:

- grants for the public-interest and community-information layer
- sponsorships and institutional underwriting for visible local operating support
- donations or memberships for community legitimacy and flexible support
- premium organization and business services for durable earned revenue
- founder capital or aligned angel support for early bridge funding and strategic flexibility

The goal is not merely to raise money. The goal is to build a funding mix that matches the actual character of the project: local infrastructure with both public-good and earned-revenue components.

## What Each Capital Source Should Fund

### Grants

Use grants to fund:

- local reporting and public-interest information work
- community outreach and trust-building
- directory and organization infrastructure that improves public access
- pilot staffing for civic/community operations
- storytelling, local history, and place-based information initiatives

Do not use grants as the permanent backbone for basic product survival.

### Sponsorships and underwriting

Use sponsorships to fund:

- recurring local operating support
- section-level underwriting
- community calendar or event visibility support
- public-facing guides or useful local resource surfaces

This is one of the best medium-term funding lanes because it is easier to explain than philanthropy and more durable than one-time donations.

### Donations and memberships

Use community support for:

- legitimacy and visible local buy-in
- modest unrestricted support
- special campaigns tied to community benefit

This should be treated as a supporting layer, not the primary engine, especially early on.

### Earned revenue

Use earned revenue to fund:

- recurring infrastructure costs
- product development
- staff and contractor support
- future expansion without constant grant dependence

This is the long-term durability layer.

### Founder capital and aligned angels

Use founder or aligned-investor capital for:

- early bridge funding
- periods between grant cycles
- setup costs before earned revenue is dependable
- preserving momentum while the blended model matures

Do not let this become a substitute for building actual recurring revenue.

## 12-Month Capital Plan

The first 12 months should be framed as a `Cambria pilot year`.

Recommended target raise:

- `Floor:` `$90,000`
- `Target:` `$150,000`
- `Stretch:` `$225,000`

### 12-month target mix

At the `Target` level of `$150,000`, the recommended blend is:

- `Grants:` `$55,000` to `$70,000`
- `Founder / aligned capital:` `$25,000` to `$40,000`
- `Sponsorships / underwriting:` `$20,000` to `$30,000`
- `Donations / memberships:` `$10,000` to `$15,000`
- `Earned revenue:` `$20,000` to `$30,000`

This implies a rough target mix of:

- `37%` to `47%` grants
- `17%` to `27%` founder or aligned capital
- `13%` to `20%` sponsorships
- `7%` to `10%` community support
- `13%` to `20%` earned revenue

### Why this mix makes sense

- it does not assume the market side is mature too early
- it does not force grants to cover everything
- it leaves room for early local buy-in
- it gives the product time to prove repeat usage before leaning heavily on earned revenue

### 12-month use of funds

The target budget should be allocated approximately as follows:

- `35%` editorial, community, and stewardship labor
- `20%` product development and technical contracting
- `15%` hosting, infrastructure, software tools, and vendor costs
- `10%` community outreach, onboarding, and local relationship work
- `10%` sales, sponsorship, and revenue-support materials
- `10%` legal, accounting, insurance, and organizational setup

## 24-Month Capital Plan

By month 24, Highlander Today should be shifting from grant-supported pilot mode toward mixed operational durability.

Recommended 24-month cumulative capital target:

- `Floor:` `$225,000`
- `Target:` `$400,000`
- `Stretch:` `$650,000`

### 24-month target mix

At the `Target` level of `$400,000`, the recommended cumulative blend is:

- `Grants:` `$120,000` to `$160,000`
- `Founder / aligned capital:` `$40,000` to `$70,000`
- `Sponsorships / underwriting:` `$70,000` to `$95,000`
- `Donations / memberships:` `$25,000` to `$40,000`
- `Earned revenue:` `$80,000` to `$110,000`

### What should change by month 24

- grants should still matter, but no longer dominate
- sponsorships should become a predictable annual line
- earned revenue should become visibly real, not experimental
- donations should function as a loyalty and legitimacy layer
- founder support should taper rather than expand

## Revenue Products To Sell Early

Do not wait for a perfect media kit or a large audience deck. Sell narrow, legible products first.

### Best early sponsorship and underwriting offers

- community calendar sponsor
- local events sponsor
- section sponsor for `Local Life`, `Help Wanted`, or `Marketplace`
- community guide sponsor
- launch sponsor for a specific neighborhood or township information push

### Best early earned-revenue offers

- managed organization page setup
- premium organization presence
- promoted event placement
- promoted help-wanted placement
- enhanced storefront presence
- flyer or printable-material generation
- memorial media hosting and richer remembrance pages

These are easier to explain than abstract subscriptions and are more aligned with actual user behavior.

## Recommended Sales Sequence

### Months 0-3

- finalize applicant structure for grants
- prepare one-page funding deck and sponsorship sheet
- begin relationship conversations with local foundations and institutional supporters
- sell the first small underwriting packages manually

### Months 3-6

- submit first wave of grants
- launch first donation or membership surface if public trust messaging is ready
- formalize 2-3 premium paid services with simple pricing
- secure 3-5 anchor sponsors or underwriters

### Months 6-12

- evaluate what actually sold without excessive friction
- expand only the revenue products that map to real demand
- convert one-off sponsors into annual supporters
- use early results to strengthen larger grant asks

### Months 12-24

- make sponsorship renewals routine
- grow premium organization and business services
- test first tenant-community licensing only after Cambria shows visible proof
- reduce dependence on bridge capital

## Source-by-Source Assessment

### Highest priority

- `Grants`
- `Local sponsorships and underwriting`
- `Premium organization and business services`

These are the strongest fit for the project as it exists now.

### Useful, but secondary

- `Donations and memberships`
- `Founder capital`

These help materially, but should not be mistaken for the long-term engine.

### Later-stage only

- `Municipal contracts`
- `Tenant community licensing`
- `Program-related investments`

These can become meaningful once the model is more mature and legible.

### Lowest-priority or high-risk

- `Traditional venture capital`
- `Conventional bank debt`
- `broad crowdfunding as a primary strategy`

These either create the wrong incentives too early or assume a level of predictability the project does not yet have.

## Operating Rules

### Rule 1

Do not hire ahead of durable revenue unless grant funds are explicitly time-bounded for that role and there is a clear off-ramp.

### Rule 2

Do not present speculative future expansion as current value when selling sponsorships or applying for grants.

### Rule 3

Every grant proposal should fund a concrete public-benefit slice, not the entire platform roadmap.

### Rule 4

Every paid local offer should answer a simple question: what does the buyer get that is obvious, useful, and locally meaningful?

### Rule 5

Track renewal potential from the start. One-time cash matters, but recurring support matters more.

## Scenario Planning

### If grant funding is slower than expected

- rely more heavily on sponsorships, founder bridge capital, and manually sold premium services
- reduce expansion assumptions
- keep the plan tightly focused on Cambria use cases

### If sponsorship sales are weaker than expected

- simplify the offers
- sell fewer packages with clearer outcomes
- focus on organizations that already spend on local visibility

### If earned revenue is weaker than expected

- audit whether the problem is pricing, packaging, or actual demand
- keep only the products people clearly understand
- do not invent more SKUs to compensate for weak fit

### If donations outperform expectations

- treat that as a trust signal
- build stronger public support and transparency surfaces
- do not assume donations alone will scale into the main operating model

## Current Conclusion

Highlander Today should be funded like a place-based institution with a software spine, not like a pure startup and not like a pure newsroom.

The recommended 12-24 month strategy is:

- use grants to establish the public-interest layer
- use sponsorships to normalize local institutional support
- use premium services to build durable recurring revenue
- use community support to reinforce legitimacy
- use founder or aligned capital only as a bridge, not as the business model
