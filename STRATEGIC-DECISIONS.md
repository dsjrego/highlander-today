## Canonical Decisions

1. Highlander Today is local digital infrastructure first, not a generic startup.

The platform should be framed as accountable, place-based community infrastructure for information, participation, discovery, and memory. If a future decision makes the project sound more like a generic software venture than a local civic/community utility, that decision should be questioned.

2. The founding community is the proving ground and long-term home base.

Cambria Heights / Cambria County is not just the first market. It is the place where the platform must prove real usefulness, trust, and repeat engagement before broader claims become credible.

3. Depth in one community matters more than breadth across many.

The core strategic test is not how many features exist or how many places can theoretically be served. The test is whether one real community begins to use the platform as part of local life.

4. Expansion is earned only after visible local adoption.

New communities, wider promotion, or larger strategic claims should follow demonstrated local usage, repeat interaction loops, and recognizable community value in the founding community.

5. Anchor before broadcast.

In the early stage, local legitimacy matters more than broad exposure. The platform should be socially anchored with real local participants, validators, and useful content before aggressive promotion or ambitious public storytelling.

6. Real local activity matters more than synthetic completeness.

The platform should prefer visible, credible local usage over empty breadth, placeholder density, or polished but unproven surfaces. Real users, organizations, and records matter more than mock fullness.

7. Trust and accountability are core differentiators, not optional garnish.

Highlander Today should continue to behave differently from anonymous or low-accountability platforms. Identity, trust levels, moderation, stewardship, and auditability are part of the product thesis, not just operational details.

8. Complete loops beat horizontal sprawl.

The platform should continue prioritizing complete, credible user loops over expanding sideways into more categories that are not yet socially validated.

9. Product exposure should be paced to what the community can absorb.

Features may be built before they are fully exposed. Gated, hidden, or soft rollout is acceptable when it helps the product mature without confusing the community or diluting attention.

10. Funding should reinforce trust, not outrun it.

Revenue, grants, donations, sponsorships, and underwriting should support the identity of the platform as community infrastructure. Funding asks should follow visible value and clear public understanding of what is being supported.

11. The home community should not feel extracted from.

Cambria Heights should remain free at the core-participation level. If paid services exist, they should map to clear value-added layers rather than charging residents for basic access to local community life.

12. Memorial and memory systems require a stricter standard than ordinary publishing.

Death notices, memorials, and related community-memory systems must remain more careful on verification, stewardship, and moderation than standard content flows.

13. The project should be able to mature into a trusted institution, not just a software product.

Governance, funding transparency, stewardship, and public legitimacy should be considered with that long-term direction in mind, even when the immediate implementation remains simple.

14. Narrative discipline matters.

The project should be described through concrete local usefulness, not through speculative scale, startup theater, or overexplained future ambition. If people understand the idea better than they understand the present value, the narrative is off-track.

15. The moat is local embeddedness, not secrecy.

The strongest defense is visible usefulness, trusted local participation, moderation quality, and community recognition. The goal is not to hide the project forever. The goal is to become the place where local digital community life actually works.

## Phase-Change Questions

Before moving into a broader strategic phase, ask:

- Is there visible real activity, not just technical readiness?
- Are recognizable local people and organizations participating?
- Are the core live loops producing repeat behavior?
- Can residents explain what Highlander Today is in plain language?
- Would a local skeptic see a real community asset or just a software project?
- Is the next move being driven by community traction or by impatience?

If the answers are weak, the right move is usually to deepen the current community rather than widen the scope.

## Summary

Highlander Today's strategy should remain simple even as the platform grows:

- prove real value in one home community
- build through trust, accountability, and visible usefulness
- pace exposure and expansion to actual adoption
- keep funding and positioning aligned with community-infrastructure identity
- avoid drifting into startup-shaped storytelling that weakens local legitimacy
