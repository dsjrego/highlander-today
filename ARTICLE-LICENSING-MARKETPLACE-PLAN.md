# Article Licensing Marketplace Plan

## Purpose

Explore a future marketplace where accountable local authors can license approved articles to organizations that need publishable local coverage. The goal is not to introduce this now, but to preserve the idea in a form that can be revisited later without losing the core reasoning.

This concept fits Highlander Today's broader direction because it could:

- create direct economic value for local writers
- help institutions and local organizations fill coverage gaps
- strengthen the platform's role as community communication infrastructure
- create a healthier business model than generic ad-driven distribution

## Current Recommendation

Do not build this yet.

The current product still needs continued focus on the core local participation loops: recurring readership, trusted interaction, visible local activity, messaging, marketplace usage, and organizational foundations. An article licensing marketplace should only be revisited once the underlying content engine and local participation patterns are clearly working.

The right near-term action is documentation and later manual validation, not product development.

## Concept Summary

An author writes a local article inside Highlander Today. After editorial approval, the article can optionally enter a marketplace where eligible organizations can purchase a license to republish it under defined terms.

In the early framing, organizations should purchase a license, not automatically buy full ownership of the article.

Examples of licensing structures:

- non-exclusive republication with required attribution
- limited local exclusivity for a time window
- syndication rights across a defined set of channels
- custom buyout only as an exceptional negotiated path

## Why This Could Matter

Small organizations often need local content but lack the time, staffing, or consistency to produce it themselves. At the same time, local writers often struggle to monetize timely, place-based reporting. A licensing marketplace could connect those two needs in a way that fits the platform's trust and accountability model.

This is especially plausible for content such as:

- school sports recaps
- municipal meeting summaries
- event coverage
- business spotlights
- community feature stories
- seasonal guides and local explainers

## Why It Should Wait

This is not a simple content feature. It is a business workflow with legal, editorial, financial, and operational consequences.

It would likely require:

- licensing terms and rights definitions
- exclusivity rules and timing controls
- editorial review and quality thresholds
- revision/version tracking after sale
- attribution requirements
- payment split and payout accounting
- dispute handling and refund policy
- buyer eligibility and organization identity rules

Introducing this before the platform's main loops are stable would risk diluting focus.

## Positioning Recommendation

When revisited, treat this as an editorial licensing marketplace, not a generic article marketplace.

That framing is better because it:

- emphasizes accountable publishing and republishing
- keeps the concept narrower than open-ended content trading
- aligns better with real organizational demand
- reduces pressure to support broad speculative creator-marketplace behavior

## Suggested Entry Point

If this ever becomes active work, phase 1 should focus on local article licensing for organizations rather than full rights trading.

Recommended first model:

- trusted or approved author submits article
- editorial/admin review approves article for licensing eligibility
- article enters a marketplace with clear license options
- eligible organizations can browse and purchase a defined license
- platform records terms, attribution requirements, and payout split

Avoid full rights buyouts as the default path. Use licensing-first language and structure.

## Dependencies Before Productization

Revisit only after most of the following are true:

- local article submission and editorial workflows are stable
- repeat local readership and visible content activity exist
- messaging and organization workflows are mature
- the organization/directory foundation exists
- payout/compliance thinking is mature enough to support creator compensation
- there is evidence that both writers and organizations want the workflow

## Validation Path

Validate manually before building product features.

Recommended manual experiment:

1. Recruit a small set of local writers.
2. Recruit a small set of organizations that might republish local stories.
3. Match a handful of stories manually.
4. Observe what they actually negotiate.

Questions to validate:

- Do buyers want exclusive or non-exclusive rights?
- Do they care more about speed, exclusivity, edits, or attribution?
- What article types are most attractive?
- What price ranges feel viable locally?
- Do authors want one-time payouts, rev-share, or flexible pricing?
- How much editorial mediation is needed?

If manual transactions do not happen naturally, the idea should remain dormant.

## Early Non-Goals

If work begins later, these should stay out of scope for the first build:

- full assignment-contract management
- broad national syndication
- automated legal negotiation
- complex bidding or auction mechanics
- native payroll/accounting infrastructure
- open marketplace access for anonymous users
- replacing the main local article experience with a rights-trading interface

## Relationship To Other Planned Systems

This idea intersects with, but should remain distinct from:

- `DIRECTORY-PLAN.md`: organizations and their accountable identity/membership model
- `QUALIFIED-INTEREST-MARKETPLACE-PLAN.md`: buyer-initiated attention/lead marketplace
- local article workflows in `Local Life`
- future reputation and analytics systems that may help measure content usefulness and buyer trust

## Decision Rule

Do not prioritize this because it sounds innovative. Prioritize it only if later evidence shows it strengthens Highlander Today's core mission of local communication, creates real value for local writers and institutions, and can be introduced without weakening focus on the main community loops.
